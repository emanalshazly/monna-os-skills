# Example 02: Arabic AI Governance Policy → English for International Stakeholders

> **Direction:** Arabic (Formal MSA) → English
> **Source Audience:** Arabic-speaking government and enterprise stakeholders in Saudi Arabia
> **Target Audience:** International English-speaking stakeholders (investors, regulators, partners)
> **Register:** Formal Arabic (MSA) → Formal English
> **Market:** Saudi Arabia (source), International (target)
> **Skill Phase Demonstrated:** All 6 phases (Source Analysis, Terminology Mapping, Cultural Adaptation, Draft, Review, Validation)

---

## Source Analysis & Register Detection (Phase 1)

**Document Type:** AI Governance Policy (internal organizational policy)
**Technical Domain:** AI governance, risk management, compliance, data protection
**Source Audience:** Saudi government and enterprise stakeholders; internal compliance teams
**Target Audience:** International stakeholders (investors, regulators, technology partners) who need to understand the organization's AI governance posture
**Source Register:** Formal Modern Standard Arabic (MSA) — government-grade formality with full Arabic technical terms
**Target Register:** Formal English — international business English, precise technical terminology, no colloquialisms

**Cultural Adaptation Notes (Arabic → English):**
- Arabic governance documents often use hierarchical, relationship-oriented framing that sounds excessive in direct English
- Arabic may imply context that is left unstated (e.g., "في إطار رؤية المملكة" assumes the reader knows Vision 2030)
- Arabic formal writing uses longer sentences with multiple clauses; English business writing prefers shorter, direct sentences
- Technical terms in Arabic may have multiple English equivalents depending on context (e.g., "الحوكمة" = governance / stewardship / oversight)
- Arabic documents may reference Saudi-specific compliance frameworks (PDPL) that need explanation for international audiences

## Cultural Adaptation (Phase 3)

### Source Context (Arabic document assumptions):
- The document assumes the reader is Saudi and understands Vision 2030 references
- The document uses hierarchical framing: "يسرنا أن نقدم" (We are pleased to present) — standard in Saudi formal writing
- The document references Saudi-specific compliance (PDPL) without explaining it for outsiders
- The tone is relationship-oriented and prestige-conscious

### Target Context (English for international stakeholders):
- International investors may not know what Vision 2030 is — add brief context
- PDPL needs explanation: "Saudi Arabia's Personal Data Protection Law (PDPL), comparable to the EU's GDPR"
- Hierarchical framing should be reduced; English business writing is more direct
- The tone should be professional but not overly deferential — international stakeholders expect confidence and clarity
- Reference international standards that international stakeholders recognize: ISO 42001, NIST AI RMF, EU AI Act

### Adaptations Made:
1. **Vision 2030 reference:** Expanded from "في إطار رؤية المملكة 2030" to "aligned with Saudi Arabia's Vision 2030 national transformation strategy"
2. **PDPL reference:** Added comparison to GDPR for international context: "PDPL (Saudi Arabia's Personal Data Protection Law, comparable in scope to the EU's GDPR)"
3. **Hierarchical openings:** Removed "يسرنا أن نقدم" (We are pleased to present) and replaced with direct statement: "This policy defines..."
4. **Implicit context:** Made explicit what was assumed in the Arabic original
5. **International standards:** Added references to ISO 42001, NIST AI RMF, and EU AI Act where the Arabic only referenced "المعايير الدولية" (international standards) vaguely

## Draft Translation (Phase 4)

### Original Arabic Source

```markdown
# سياسة حوكمة الذكاء الاصطناعي

## المقدمة

يسرنا أن نقدم لسيادتكم سياسة حوكمة الذكاء الاصطناعي، والتي تم وضعها في إطار رؤية المملكة العربية السعودية 2030، وتهدف إلى ضمان استخدام الذكاء الاصطناعي بما يتوافق مع المعايير الدولية ونظام حماية البيانات الشخصية (PDPL) ومعايير الجودة المعتمدة.

تغطي هذه السياسة جميع نماذج التعلم الآلي المستخدمة في العمليات التشغيلية، وتتضمن إجراءات التقييم الدوري للمخاطر التشغيلية والتقنية والتنظيمية المرتبطة بتطبيقات الذكاء الاصطناعي.

## أهداف الحوكمة

1. ضمان جودة البيانات وموثوقيتها في جميع مصادر البيانات المستخدمة لتدريب النماذج.
2. ضمان الشفافية والمساءلة في اتخاذ القرارات المبنية على نماذج التعلم الآلي.
3. ضمان الإنصاف وقابلية التفسير في النتائج المخرجة من النماذج.
4. الالتزام بالأنظمة واللوائح المحلية والدولية المتعلقة بحماية البيانات والخصوصية.

## إدارة المخاطر

تخضع جميع نماذج التعلم الآلي لتقييم دوري للمخاطر يشمل:
- المخاطر التشغيلية: التأثير على العمليات التجارية والاستمرارية.
- المخاطر التقنية: أداء النموذج ودقة التنبؤات والثغرات الأمنية.
- المخاطر التنظيمية: الالتزام بـ PDPL والمعايير الدولية.

يتم إجراء التدقيق الدوري كل ستة أشهر، ويتم رفع التقارير إلى لجنة الحوكمة العليا.
```

### English Translation (Formal International Business English)

```markdown
# AI Governance Policy

## Introduction

This policy defines the organization's framework for artificial intelligence (AI) governance, established in alignment with Saudi Arabia's Vision 2030 national transformation strategy. It ensures that all AI applications comply with international standards, Saudi Arabia's Personal Data Protection Law (PDPL) — comparable in scope to the EU's General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) — and certified quality benchmarks.

The policy covers all machine learning models deployed in operational environments, and includes procedures for periodic risk assessment of operational, technical, and regulatory risks associated with AI applications.

## Governance Objectives

1. Ensure data quality and reliability across all data sources used for model training.
2. Ensure transparency and accountability in decisions made using machine learning models.
3. Ensure fairness and explainability in model outputs and predictions.
4. Maintain compliance with local and international regulations governing data protection and privacy.

## Risk Management

All machine learning models are subject to periodic risk assessment covering:
- **Operational risks:** Impact on business operations and continuity.
- **Technical risks:** Model performance, prediction accuracy, and security vulnerabilities.
- **Regulatory risks:** Compliance with PDPL and applicable international standards, including ISO 42001 and the NIST AI Risk Management Framework (AI RMF).

Audits are conducted every six months. Findings are reported to the senior governance committee.
```

## Translation Decisions & Rationale

### 1. "يسرنا أن نقدم لسيادتكم" → "This policy defines..."
- **Arabic:** "We are pleased to present to Your Excellencies..." (hierarchical, deferential)
- **English:** Direct statement. International stakeholders do not expect deferential language from organizational policy documents. Confidence and clarity are valued over hierarchy.
- **Rationale:** Cultural adaptation — removed hierarchical framing that is appropriate in Saudi context but excessive in international business English.

### 2. "في إطار رؤية المملكة العربية السعودية 2030" → "in alignment with Saudi Arabia's Vision 2030 national transformation strategy"
- **Arabic:** "Within the framework of the Kingdom of Saudi Arabia's Vision 2030" (assumes reader knows what Vision 2030 is)
- **English:** Added "national transformation strategy" to provide context for international readers who may not be familiar.
- **Rationale:** Explicit context for international audience. The Arabic original assumes local knowledge.

### 3. "نظام حماية البيانات الشخصية (PDPL)" → "Saudi Arabia's Personal Data Protection Law (PDPL) — comparable in scope to the EU's GDPR"
- **Arabic:** References PDPL with no explanation (assumes Saudi reader knows)
- **English:** Added full name and GDPR comparison for international regulatory context.
- **Rationale:** International stakeholders need to understand the regulatory landscape. Comparing to GDPR creates a familiar anchor.

### 4. "المخاطر التشغيلية والتقنية والتنظيمية" → "operational, technical, and regulatory risks"
- **Arabic:** Lists three risk categories
- **English:** Same three categories, but added structured bullet points with bold headers for readability
- **Rationale:** English business documents value scannable structure. Arabic formal documents often use paragraph lists.

### 5. "يتم إجراء التدقيق الدوري كل ستة أشهر، ويتم رفع التقارير إلى لجنة الحوكمة العليا" → "Audits are conducted every six months. Findings are reported to the senior governance committee."
- **Arabic:** One long sentence with passive voice ("يتم إجراء... ويتم رفع...")
- **English:** Two short, active sentences
- **Rationale:** English business writing prefers active voice and shorter sentences. Arabic formal writing uses longer, more complex sentences with multiple clauses.

### 6. "المعايير الدولية" → "international standards, including ISO 42001 and the NIST AI Risk Management Framework (AI RMF)"
- **Arabic:** Vague reference to "international standards"
- **English:** Named specific standards that international stakeholders recognize
- **Rationale:** International audience needs concrete references, not vague generalities. This also demonstrates the organization's maturity to international partners.

## Key Takeaways from This Example

1. **Arabic → English is reconstruction, not translation.** Arabic formal documents often imply context, use hierarchical framing, and prefer long compound sentences. English business documents require explicit context, direct tone, and shorter sentences. The translator must reconstruct, not just translate words.

2. **Cultural framing must be adapted.** "يسرنا أن نقدم" (We are pleased to present) is standard and respectful in Saudi Arabic. In English international business writing, it sounds excessive. The translator must judge what cultural framing to preserve and what to adapt.

3. **Implicit context must be made explicit.** Arabic documents written for local audiences assume shared knowledge (Vision 2030, PDPL). International audiences need this context explained. The translator acts as a cultural bridge, not just a language converter.

4. **Technical terms have precision requirements.** "الإنصاف" could be "fairness," "equity," or "justice." In AI governance, "fairness" is the established technical term. The translator must know the technical domain, not just the dictionary.

5. **Vague references need concrete anchors.** "المعايير الدولية" (international standards) is vague in Arabic. For an international audience, naming ISO 42001 and NIST AI RMF demonstrates credibility and provides concrete anchors for understanding.

6. **Sentence structure differs significantly.** Arabic formal writing uses passive voice and long sentences with multiple clauses. English business writing prefers active voice and shorter sentences. This is a structural difference, not just a vocabulary one.
